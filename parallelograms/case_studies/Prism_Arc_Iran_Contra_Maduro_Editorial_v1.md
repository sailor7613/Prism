## Event 1 — Iran-Contra / Didion (1982–1987)

**Framing:** In 1982, Joan Didion traveled to El Salvador. What she found was a country where the U.S. embassy's language had detached entirely from the reality outside its walls — bodies left in public spaces, a military apparatus operating with American support and without American scrutiny. Her account wasn't investigative or polemical. It was observational, intuitive — she described the distance between what the State Department said was happening and what was actually in front of her. Years later, it was revealed that the Reagan administration had been covertly selling arms to Iran and funneling the proceeds to Contra rebels in Nicaragua, in direct violation of the Boland Amendment's congressional prohibition. The gap Didion saw on the ground was the visible surface of a covert institutional operation that wouldn't be named for years.

## Event 2 — IRCA + IIRIRA (1986/1996)

**Framing:** Two landmark immigration bills, ten years apart, both passed with broad bipartisan support. IRCA in 1986 offered amnesty to roughly three million undocumented immigrants while creating the employer sanctions framework. IIRIRA in 1996 created the three-year and ten-year bars — leaving the country after unlawful presence triggered a three- or ten-year ban on legal re-entry. The bars transformed unauthorized presence from a temporary condition into a permanent one. Together, the two bills built the architectural foundation of the modern immigration crisis. The system was designed to produce the problem it claimed to address.

**Prevalent axis:** Y
**X:** Enforcement-first (right) ↔ Integration-first (left)
**Y:** Legislative architecture (institutional) ↔ Lived experience (grassroots)

- Object Z: **+0.90 (realized)** — IRCA regularized a labor force without dismantling the conditions that produced unauthorized entry. IIRIRA locked that labor force in place permanently. The system needs the gap.
- Dia: 70/75 — Pattern #16, Z-gap by architectural design

## Event 5 — Syrian Civil War / Refugee Crisis (2011–2016)

**Framing:** U.S. and allied intervention in the Syrian civil war contributed to the destabilization of a country of 22 million people. The resulting displacement — the largest refugee crisis since World War II — sent millions into Europe and hundreds of thousands toward the Americas. The European refugee crisis became the single most potent accelerant of right-wing populism across the Western world. In the U.S., images of Syrian displacement landed directly in the 2016 presidential campaign and supercharged the border security narrative. This is the arc's tangential event — not about U.S. immigration policy directly — but it surfaces the imperial template at its clearest: intervention destabilizes a country, displacement produces a refugee crisis, the crisis becomes domestic political fuel, and that fuel justifies further intervention or restriction.
