# PROJECTION 2 — Parallelogram Article

## The Floor That Everybody Owns

### Part I: The Architecture

In 2012, with the foreclosure wave still cresting, the Federal Reserve was holding interest rates near zero. Capital was abundant and yield was scarce. Invitation Homes — a subsidiary of Blackstone — had a solution. Over the next five years, they and a small number of competitors purchased more than 300,000 single-family homes out of foreclosure across the Sun Belt, converting owner-occupied properties to rentals and beginning the systematic institutional management of a housing stock that American families had historically owned.

The strategy was enabled by a specific market moment: a Federal Reserve explicitly trying to reflate asset prices, a foreclosure inventory priced below replacement cost, and a regulatory environment that treated bulk residential purchases as ordinary private transactions with no disclosure requirements. No law was broken. The architecture was built from available materials.

RealPage followed. Its algorithmic rent-setting platform — used by Invitation Homes and most of its competitors — allowed institutional landlords to price units using data aggregated across competitor portfolios. A federal antitrust investigation later found that this produced coordinated pricing that exceeded what competitive markets would have generated, without the landlords ever exchanging an email. The algorithm coordinated them. No price-fixing meeting required.

The bill to restrict institutional ownership of single-family homes attracted bipartisan co-sponsors. It had support from progressive housing advocates, from populist-right legislators responding to constituent anger, from small landlord associations who understood what bulk buying did to acquisition prices. It died in committee. The finance lobby spent more than any of the supporting coalitions combined.

### Part II: The YIMBY Problem

The supply constraint is real. Single-family exclusion zoning, parking requirements, minimum lot sizes, and decade-long permit delays have suppressed housing construction in the highest-demand markets for fifty years. This is not the whole story of why rents are high, but it is part of the story, and the part that good-faith supply-siders identified early and correctly.

What happened to that argument is instructive.

YIMBY — Yes In My Back Yard — emerged as a deliberate counter to NIMBY around 2013–2015 in San Francisco. The original movement had genuine progressive credentials: density advocates, urbanists, transit riders, people who understood that exclusionary zoning was a mechanism for racial and economic segregation dressed in neighborhood-character language. They were right about this. Single-family zoning in American cities has a documented history of racially restrictive enforcement. The opposition to density is, in many places, a class-and-race coalition defending property values against people who cannot afford them.

But the counter-acronym carried a structural problem from its inception. NIMBY had thirty years of uncontested use before YIMBY appeared. The moment the counter-term formed, the dialectic locked. Both positions became denominatable — NIMBY as the retrograde obstructionist, YIMBY as the technocratic optimist — and the political work of either position collapsed into tribal signaling. More importantly, the YIMBY argument's institutional orientation (+Y: the solution is regulatory reform, zoning change, policy engagement) began attracting a coalition of stakeholders who were interested in deregulation as a mechanism for luxury development, not workforce housing. "Build more" became indistinguishable from "build whatever the market will bear where the margins are highest."

### Part III: The Intra-Positional Tension

The most important analytical feature of housing as a Y-prevalent event is the intra-positional dialectical tension it produces within the -Y left quadrant.

The tenant union organizer and the NIMBY neighbor are not natural allies. The tenant union organizer is -Y left: skeptical of institutional remedies, committed to direct action and community infrastructure, fundamentally opposed to the commodification of housing. The NIMBY neighbor is -Y right: skeptical of institutional change, committed to community continuity, fundamentally opposed to the disruption of established residential patterns. They appear to share a -Y framing — community over institution, local over federal, existing relationships over imposed change — and on the specific question of corporate bulk purchasing, they do share it. Both are losing to the same thing.

But the NIMBY position carries a concealed +Y structure. Opposition to new development does not build community infrastructure — it preserves existing property values. The defense of "community character" is frequently a defense of the class composition and racial composition of an existing community against the people who cannot currently afford to live in it. The tenant union organizer who builds a genuine community land trust is doing something structurally different from the homeowner association that opposes density to protect property values, even if both use the language of community defense.

The coalition response from -Y left holds this honestly: the tenant union organizer who ran three successful rent rollbacks is watching community preservation language on her own side slide toward something she doesn't entirely recognize. That slide is the diagnostic. When "protect our community" stops meaning "build the infrastructure that makes this a good place to live" and starts meaning "prevent people unlike us from living here," the vehicle has consumed the object.

### Part IV: The Chain Nobody Holds

The housing crisis has a causal depth of approximately ten links. The 2008 financial crisis produced a foreclosure wave. The foreclosure wave produced suppressed acquisition prices at the moment zero-rate Federal Reserve policy was flooding capital markets with liquidity seeking yield. That combination made bulk single-family purchasing rational for institutional actors with patient capital. Institutional ownership concentration made algorithmic rent coordination possible. Algorithmic coordination produced rents above competitive market levels in supply-constrained markets. Supply constraints were produced and maintained by fifty years of zoning policy driven by homeowner lobbies protecting property values. Post-2022 interest rate increases made homeownership mathematically impossible for a generation of would-be buyers, converting them permanently to renters. Record corporate landlord profits accrued during a period of record tenant cost burden. The legislative response failed because the lobbying infrastructure of institutional real estate finance outspent the coalitions trying to reform it.

Low-Diatribe users hold seven or eight of these links simultaneously. High-Diatribe users on the left hold two: corporate greed, government capture. High-Diatribe users on the right hold two: zoning restrictions, government interference. In both cases, the amputation is at the point where the causal chain stops being attributable to the opposition and starts requiring an account of how one's own side's institutional failures contributed to the architecture.

The right amputates before "Federal Reserve policy" because that's where the story of cheap capital driving asset inflation begins, and cheap capital driving asset inflation is not obviously a Democrat story. The left amputates before "zoning boards" because that's where the story of progressive homeowner lobbies blocking density begins, and NIMBY is not obviously a Republican story.

What both sides lose at the amputation point is the same thing: an accurate account of how the housing crisis was built, by whom, over how long, and what that means for what could actually fix it. That accurate account is what Prism is designed to hold visible.
