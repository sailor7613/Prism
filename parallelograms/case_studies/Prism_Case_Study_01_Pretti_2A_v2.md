# PROJECTION 2 — Parallelogram Article

## The Night Everybody Lost the Object

### Part I: What Happened

On the morning of January 24, 2026, Alex Pretti — a thirty-seven-year-old intensive care nurse at the Minneapolis VA — walked less than two miles from his home to a stretch of Nicollet Avenue where Border Patrol agents were conducting an immigration arrest near a doughnut shop. He was carrying his legally holstered firearm and filming with his phone. Within six minutes, he would be pepper-sprayed, thrown to the ground, and shot ten times by two agents who have since been identified but whose names the federal government withheld from Congress and state investigators.

The administration's response was immediate. DHS Secretary Kristi Noem called it "domestic terrorism." Assistant Secretary Tricia McLaughlin said Pretti wanted to "massacre law enforcement." The Minneapolis police chief said he'd received no cooperation from federal investigators. A state judge issued a restraining order to preserve evidence. The FBI refused to share anything with the state Bureau of Criminal Apprehension.

Within forty-eight hours, bystander video from six angles had been verified by Reuters, the BBC, the Wall Street Journal, and ABC News. The footage showed a man holding up a phone, stepping between an agent and a woman on the ground, being sprayed, being wrestled down by multiple agents, and being shot while on the ground. No verified footage shows him reaching for or holding his weapon.

At a Diatribe reading near zero, the event is at its most legible. From every quadrant, the basic facts hold: an American citizen was killed by federal agents, the official account was contradicted by video, and the investigation was obstructed. The institutional left demands independent investigation. The institutional right demands credibility and accountability. The grassroots left sees a neighbor. The grassroots right sees a gun owner whose constitutional rights were violated. These positions are not identical — they carry different priorities and different frameworks — but they can see the same object.

### Part II: The Bilateral Inversion

What makes this event a stress test for political analysis is what happened to the Second Amendment.

For decades, the American right has argued that the Second Amendment exists to protect citizens against state tyranny. The NRA's foundational narrative — going back to Wayne LaPierre's "jack-booted government thugs" letter in the 1990s — is that an armed citizenry is the last check on a government that has turned against its own people. Alex Pretti was doing exactly what that narrative describes: a legally armed citizen, present at a confrontation between federal agents and civilians, exercising his right to observe and intervene.

The institutional right fractured. The NRA, the Minnesota Gun Owners Caucus, Thomas Massie, and bipartisan senators held the principled position: carrying a firearm is a constitutional right, not a justification for lethal force. But the Trump administration — Noem, Bovino, Essayli, Miller — abandoned 2A principles entirely, arguing that carrying a weapon near federal agents made you a legitimate target. A Trump-appointed U.S. Attorney said that if you approach law enforcement with a gun, you will be "legally justified in being shot."

The left inverted simultaneously. The Liberal Gun Club's membership jumped from 2,700 to 4,500. Former Representative Dean Phillips publicly apologized to 2A defenders. Armed men showed up at the Pretti vigil invoking Che Guevara. Progressives who had spent years arguing against gun proliferation began buying firearms and organizing armed community defense.

In the coalition zone, the responses capture something the good-faith and bad-faith variants cannot: the *experience of watching your own side lose the object.* The institutional-left voice notices the jump from "independent inquiry" to "abolish ICE" and worries about the people in the middle. The institutional-right voice watches its own side call a legal gun carrier a terrorist and realizes something has been lost that's more important than any policy argument. The grassroots-left voice went to the vigil, felt the impulse to arm up, and is scared — not of the state but of the speed at which the discourse moved past the harder questions. The grassroots-right voice, a lifelong Second Amendment voter, watches people on their own side explain why this killing was acceptable and recognizes tribalism wearing 2A as a costume.

This is denomination in transit. The object (what happened to Alex Pretti) is still visible but the vehicles are forming around it. Within days, the event will be fully consumed — "domestic terrorism" on one side, "fascist execution" on the other — and the man who walked two miles from his home to help a stranger will be invisible.

### Part III: The Questions Nobody Asks

Eleven days before the shooting, a video shows Alex Pretti at another ICE protest. He's in an altercation with agents. As they get into an SUV, he kicks out the taillight of their vehicle. They tackle him.

The right immediately weaponized this: proof he was an agitator. The left immediately dismissed it: irrelevant character assassination. Neither side asked the questions the video actually raises.

Was Pretti targeted after the taillight incident? Did the agents who shot him know who he was? Was there an institutional response to the earlier confrontation that put him on a list or a lookout?

Was Pretti part of an organizing network? Is there evidence of coordinated community defense emerging in Minneapolis — a "charged No Kings" — and if so, what does that mean for how we understand his presence that morning?

These questions don't justify the shooting. Nothing in the taillight video makes it acceptable to shoot a man on the ground ten times. But the refusal to ask them — from both sides, for opposite reasons — is itself a diagnostic. The right can't ask "was he targeted?" because the answer might implicate ICE in something worse than a bad shoot. The left can't ask "was he organizing?" because the answer might complicate the narrative of a simple bystander.

Good faith can hold both. Alex Pretti may have been deliberately organizing against ICE, and his killing was still unjustified. These are not in tension. The refusal to hold both is the signature of denomination — the point where the object disappears and the vehicle takes its place.

"Time to arm up" faces off against "play stupid games, win stupid prizes." The man, the video, the questions — all gone. Replaced by vehicles that serve tribal functions without carrying any of the substance that made the event matter in the first place.

### Part IV: The Chain Nobody Holds

The deepest analytical challenge of the Pretti event is its causal depth. The chain runs: U.S. foreign policy in the Horn of Africa → displacement of Somali populations → immigration and community formation in Minnesota → welfare system design flaws → pandemic benefit expansion → exploitation and fraud → state paralysis over racism accusations → federal weaponization of fraud narrative → Operation Metro Surge → escalation through Renee Good's killing → Pretti's killing.

No one holds the whole chain. A user at low Diatribe might hold six or seven links. The Diatribe measures where the chain gets amputated — where the user decides that the causes before their cut-point are irrelevant and the effects after it are someone else's fault.

The right amputates at "fraud" — everything before it is context, everything after it is consequence. The left amputates at "displacement" — everything before it is imperial guilt, everything after it is state violence. The object that both sides lose is the same: a community of human beings navigating a situation they didn't create, containing both legitimate grievance and real misconduct, deserving of both accountability and protection simultaneously.

That simultaneity is what Prism is designed to hold visible.
